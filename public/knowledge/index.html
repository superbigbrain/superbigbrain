Terminology

Background Knowledge

Also, basic, foundational, fundamental, or required knowledge.

Every field has a set of knowledge that is considered table stakes. Knowing this stuff does not make you an expert, but not knowing it means you’re certainly not an expert.

By way of example, the inverted index data structure is considered background or foundational knowledge in the information retrieval field. Inverted indexes are so common and so fundamental to the field that you can’t be considered an expert without understanding them.

When learning a new field, your primary goal should be to establish strong background knowledge on the field. It’s important that you have strong foundational knowledge if your goal is to eventually do important work.

Q & A

I want to become an expert on information retrieval and natural language processing. How do I do that?

Information retrieval and natural language processing are both huge fields. Each one has a set of foundational or background knowledge that is required to be considered an “expert”. Your job is to discover and learn that background knowledge. You will know you’re successful when you can have an intelligent conversation about the state of information retrieval and natural language processing with someone who is a recognized expert.

See Background Knowledge for more information.

How does one acquire background knowledge?

Good question!

For the sake of simplicity, let’s assume that the knowledge you want to pick up already exists. Your job is to find it. That means identifying useful source materials such as papers and books.

What makes a source useful?

Well, that really depends on what your goal is. For now, let’s say that our goal is to know the names of things.

Why is our goal to know the names of things?

Because you don’t know anything right now and you don’t know what you don’t know. Our first order of business is to grow the list of known-unknowns: the set of things you know exist, but don’t yet understand.

When you know the names of things, then you can ask questions about those things. This is how we move things from unknown-unknown to known-unknown.

OK. What makes a source useful if our goal is to know the names of things?

What we’re looking for is a broad overview of the field. Wikipedia is usually a good place to start.

After Wikipedia, other places that should have relatively good diversity are the Papers We Love Github repository and the most downloaded and cited papers from the relevant ACM SIG.

Each of these sources should reference other sources that might be useful. A source if useful if it helps you grow your terminology with respect to the field you’re studying.

And what artifacts should result from reading these sources?

One good artifact to have is a terminology document that explains the key ideas and themes of the field at a high level. Anki cards are even better, because spaced repition seems to be a good idea.

You should also have a list of questions that came to you while reading the source material. These could be questions about things that weren’t explained well or things that weren’t explained at all or anything else. Not all of these questions will ultimately be relevant to building your background knowledge, but it’s good to err on the side of having too many questions than too few.

  • Q: What about a knowledge graph?

And what do I do after I have these things?

By the time you have the terminology down and a reasonable set of questions, you should be competent enough to start tracking down answers to your questions. Your hunt for answers will lead to more questions, and so you’ll gradually find yourself answering more and more advanced questions.

How do I tell if I understand something?

Write about it. Explain it to someone else. Writing is thinking and forces you to clarify what you mean. Holes in your story become obvious when the story is written down. Writing allows you the opportunity to synthesize all of the knowledge you’ve accumulated into some coherent and inherently useful.

What else should I keep in mind?

  • Force yourself to justify your beliefs. Why must this thing be true? What exactly is this thing?

  • Concrete examples are worth their weight in gold. Much time is lost to abstract examples. Being concrete makes it easier to be simple, clear, and consise and to avoid hand-waving over important implementation details.
